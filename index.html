What the score means

Each city gets a number from 0 to 100. It tells you how well this week's weather (the heat, the humidity and the rain) suits mosquitoes that want to breed. A low number means the weather is working against them. A high number means conditions are about as good as they get. And remember, this is all about the weather. It is not a count of cases, and it says nothing about your own risk of getting sick.

How we work out the score

We show exactly how each score is worked out, so anyone can check it or adjust it. It comes from four things about the weather, combined like this:

Score = 100 × temperature fit × ( 0.30 humidity + 0.25 recent rain + 0.45 earlier rain )

Temperature acts like a gatekeeper. If it's too cold or too hot, mosquitoes won't breed no matter how wet it is, so the score drops close to zero. Once the temperature lands in their comfort zone, the rest comes down to how much standing water and humidity there is.

There's one twist with rain: it helps, but only up to a point. A heavy downpour can wash eggs and larvae out of the puddles they live in, so a real deluge actually pulls the score down rather than pushing it up.

Grounded in published research

This follows a well-established line of Indian research that maps mosquito-borne disease risk by combining environmental layers (water, moisture, vegetation, temperature) with transparent weights. Ours is a lightweight, daily version that uses recent weather in place of satellite imagery. For example, Palo and colleagues (2025) mapped risk zones in Cuttack, Odisha; Ali and Ahmad (2019) used a GIS and weighted-overlay method for Kolkata; and Kumar and Agrawal (2020) mapped vector habitats in Gorakhpur. The temperature band follows Mordecai and colleagues (2017), who found that Aedes-borne transmission runs roughly from 18 to 34°C and peaks around 26 to 29°C.

Where the data comes from, and what it can't tell you

Where the weather comes from

Weather data is pulled for each city by its location.

Where it falls short

  • It only looks at the weather. It doesn't count real mosquitoes, infections or cases.
  • It's tuned for the mosquitoes behind dengue and chikungunya (Aedes). Malaria, Japanese encephalitis and filariasis come from other mosquitoes that breed in different places, so treat those as only loosely covered until we add dedicated modules.
  • We take one reading near the middle of each city, so very local spots like building sites or stored water won't show up.
  • Treat the score as a rough guide, not an exact measurement.
  • One more time: the air temperature here is just weather. It is not a fever.

Why we split it into three

Layer 1, the one you're looking at, is all about the weather. Layer 2, further down this page, watches how often people search for fever and symptoms (on sample data for now, until we connect a live search source). Layer 3, at the bottom, brings in the outbreaks that India's Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP) actually reports each week, read straight from its official weekly PDF. We keep the three apart instead of rolling them into one number, because that is more honest and easier to trust.

What this is

This tracks how often people across India look up fever and symptom terms, shown for the same cities as the weather layer and over the last few weeks. Search interest is only reliable at the state level here, so each city shows its state's signal. It is a sign of attention, not a count of cases, and the values are relative on a 0 to 100 scale. It stands on its own. It is not added to the weather score above, and the air temperature in Layer 1 has nothing to do with a body-temperature fever.

Search interest is relative and normalised, not a tally of people or of cases. A single news story can lift searches everywhere at once. Read it next to official surveillance, never as a diagnosis.

What this is

This is the official ground truth: outbreaks that India's Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme has actually reported for the week shown. It is the last link in the chain, the one that confirms what the weather and the searches hinted at. It lags by a week or two and it undercounts, because not every illness becomes a reported outbreak. It confirms, it does not predict, and it stands on its own. It is not added to the weather score or the search signal above.
